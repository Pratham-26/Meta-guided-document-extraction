CYBERSECURITY ASSESSMENT AND COMPLIANCE SERVICES AGREEMENT

This Cybersecurity Assessment and Compliance Services Agreement (the "Agreement"), bearing
reference number CSA-2025-0112, is entered into as of February 1, 2025 (the "Effective Date"),
by and between the parties identified in Section 1 below.

WHEREAS, the financial services sector has become the most heavily targeted industry for
cyberattacks, with the volume and sophistication of threats directed at banking institutions
increasing at an alarming rate over the past decade. Financial institutions, by virtue of the
sensitive nature of the data they process and the critical role they play in the global economic
infrastructure, present an exceptionally attractive target for a broad spectrum of threat actors,
ranging from financially motivated criminal enterprises and state-sponsored advanced persistent
threat (APT) groups to opportunistic cybercriminals exploiting known vulnerabilities at scale.
The proliferation of digital banking channels, mobile payment platforms, real-time payment
networks, and open banking application programming interfaces (APIs) has dramatically expanded
the attack surface available to adversaries, creating new vectors for unauthorized access, data
exfiltration, and fraudulent transaction processing;

WHEREAS, the regulatory landscape governing cybersecurity in the financial services industry has
undergone substantial expansion and maturation in response to the escalating threat environment.
The Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (GLBA) and its implementing Safeguards Rule, as amended by the
Federal Trade Commission in 2021, impose comprehensive information security program requirements
on financial institutions, including designated qualified individual oversight, risk assessments,
access controls, encryption, multi-factor authentication, and incident response planning. The
Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX) establishes internal control requirements for publicly traded companies
that encompass information technology general controls (ITGCs) and application controls directly
relevant to financial reporting systems. The Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard (PCI
DSS), version 4.0, introduces enhanced requirements for targeted risk analysis, continuous
authentication mechanisms, and expanded detection and monitoring capabilities. Concurrently,
federal banking regulators, including the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC), the
Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC), and the Federal Reserve Board, have issued
heightened examination expectations through the Cybersecurity and Resilience Advanced Notice of
Proposed Rulemaking (ANPR) and updated IT Examination Handbook booklets on information security,
business continuity planning, and outsourced technology services;

WHEREAS, the evolution of attack methodologies has outpaced many institutions' defensive
capabilities, with threat actors increasingly leveraging artificial intelligence and machine
learning to automate reconnaissance, craft more convincing phishing campaigns, develop polymorphic
malware capable of evading traditional signature-based detection, and identify zero-day
vulnerabilities in widely deployed enterprise software platforms. Ransomware attacks against the
financial services sector have grown in both frequency and severity, with attackers employing
double and triple extortion tactics combining data encryption with threats of public data
leakage and regulatory reporting. Supply chain attacks, exemplified by incidents targeting
managed security service providers, network management tools, and open-source software
dependencies, have demonstrated the potential for a single compromise to cascade across hundreds
or thousands of downstream organizations. Business email compromise (BEC) schemes continue to
result in billions of dollars in annual losses, exploiting the inherent trust relationships
within financial organizations and their correspondent banking networks;

WHEREAS, the convergence of information technology (IT) and operational technology (OT) within
banking environments, driven by branch modernization initiatives, smart building systems, and
the deployment of Internet of Things (IoT) devices for environmental monitoring and physical
access control, has introduced additional complexity to the cybersecurity risk landscape. These
converged environments require assessment approaches that account for both traditional IT
vulnerabilities and OT-specific risks, including the potential for cyber-physical attacks
targeting critical banking infrastructure. The increasing adoption of cloud computing
infrastructure, containerization, and serverless architectures for core banking applications has
further complicated the security assessment process, requiring specialized expertise in cloud
security configurations, identity and access management (IAM) policies, and multi-tenant
isolation controls;

WHEREAS, social engineering remains one of the most effective and prevalent attack vectors
against financial institutions, with phishing, spear-phishing, pretexting, and voice phishing
(vishing) attacks consistently ranking among the top initial access vectors identified in
industry threat intelligence reports. The effectiveness of social engineering attacks is
amplified within the banking sector due to the hierarchical nature of financial organizations,
the availability of publicly accessible information about institutional structure and personnel,
and the inherent urgency associated with financial transactions that enables attackers to
circumvent normal verification procedures through manufactured time-pressure scenarios;

WHEREAS, the parties desire to enter into a comprehensive cybersecurity assessment and
compliance advisory arrangement that addresses the foregoing considerations and establishes a
framework for the systematic identification, evaluation, and remediation of cybersecurity risks
facing the Client's information systems, data assets, and regulatory compliance posture;

NOW, THEREFORE, in consideration of the mutual covenants, promises, and agreements set forth
herein, and for other good and valuable consideration, the receipt and sufficiency of which
are hereby acknowledged, the parties agree as follows:

2. DEFINITIONS

2.1 "Attack Vector" means any method, pathway, or technique used by a Threat Actor to gain
unauthorized access to, compromise, or disrupt the Client's information systems, networks,
applications, or data assets, including but not limited to network-based attacks, application-
layer exploits, social engineering techniques, physical access breaches, and supply chain
compromises.

2.2 "CISA" means the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency, a component of the
United States Department of Homeland Security responsible for cybersecurity coordination,
threat intelligence dissemination, and critical infrastructure protection.

2.3 "Common Vulnerability Scoring System" or "CVSS" means the industry-standard framework
published by FIRST (Forum of Incident Response and Security Teams) for rating the severity
of security vulnerabilities, providing a numerical score ranging from 0.0 to 10.0 based on
exploitability metrics and impact metrics.

2.4 "Control Framework" means a structured set of cybersecurity policies, procedures, and
technical controls organized according to an industry-recognized taxonomy, including without
limitation the NIST Cybersecurity Framework, the CIS Controls (formerly SANS Top 20), the
ISO/IEC 27001 standard, and the COBIT framework.

2.5 "CVE" means Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures, a dictionary-style catalog of publicly
known cybersecurity vulnerabilities and exposures maintained by MITRE Corporation under the
sponsorship of the National Cybersecurity Center of Excellence (NCCoE) at NIST.

2.6 "Exploit" means a piece of software, a technique, or a sequence of commands that takes
advantage of a vulnerability in a system, application, or protocol to cause unintended or
unanticipated behavior, including unauthorized data access, privilege escalation, denial of
service, or remote code execution.

2.7 "GLBA" means the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (15 U.S.C. Sections 6801-6809), including the
GLBA Privacy Rule implementing Regulation P and the GLBA Safeguards Rule implementing
Regulation S-P, as each may be amended from time to time.

2.8 "Incident Response" means the organized approach to addressing and managing the aftermath
of a security breach or cyberattack, including identification, containment, eradication,
recovery, and post-incident analysis activities conducted in accordance with a documented
incident response plan.

2.9 "NIST" means the National Institute of Standards and Technology, a non-regulatory agency
of the United States Department of Commerce, and includes all publications, special
publications, and frameworks issued by NIST relevant to cybersecurity, including NIST
Special Publication 800-53 (Security and Privacy Controls), NIST Special Publication 800-115
(Technical Guide to Information Security Testing and Assessment), and the NIST Cybersecurity
Framework (CSF) version 2.0.

2.10 "PCI DSS" means the Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard, a set of security
standards established by the Payment Card Industry Security Standards Council (PCI SSC) to
protect cardholder data, as further described in the current version of the standard and
related supporting documentation.

2.11 "Penetration Test" or "Pentest" means an authorized simulated cyberattack performed on
the Client's information systems, networks, or applications to evaluate the security posture
of the target environment by attempting to exploit vulnerabilities using the same tools,
techniques, and processes as an adversary, conducted in accordance with the Rules of
Engagement set forth in Exhibit A.

2.12 "Remediation" means the process of identifying, developing, implementing, and verifying
corrective actions to address vulnerabilities, security gaps, and compliance deficiencies
identified during the performance of assessment services under this Agreement.

2.13 "Risk Score" means a numerical or categorical rating assigned to identified
vulnerabilities, threats, or control deficiencies based on the likelihood of exploitation and
the potential impact on the Client's business operations, data assets, regulatory compliance,
and reputation, calculated using the proprietary risk scoring methodology described in
Exhibit B.

2.14 "SOC" means System and Organization Controls, a suite of audit reports issued by an
independent certified public accounting firm on controls relevant to security, availability,
processing integrity, confidentiality, and privacy at service organizations, including SOC 1
(SSAE 18) and SOC 2 reports.

2.15 "SOX" means the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 (Pub. L. 107-204, 116 Stat. 745), including
the requirements established by Section 302 and Section 404 regarding management
certification of internal controls over financial reporting and auditor attestation of
internal control effectiveness.

2.16 "Threat Actor" means any individual, group, organization, or entity that poses a
potential risk to the Client's information security, including but not limited to external
threat actors such as criminal organizations, state-sponsored groups, hacktivists, and
terrorist organizations, as well as internal threat actors such as disgruntled employees,
negligent insiders, and compromised accounts.

2.17 "Vulnerability" means a weakness, flaw, or deficiency in an information system, network,
application, or process that could be exploited by a Threat Actor to compromise the
confidentiality, integrity, or availability of the Client's data or systems, as classified
according to severity (Critical, High, Medium, Low, or Informational) in accordance with the
Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS).

2.18 "Zero-Day" means a vulnerability in software, hardware, or firmware that is unknown to
the vendor and for which no patch or remediation is available at the time of discovery or
exploitation, representing a particularly dangerous category of security risk due to the
absence of available defensive countermeasures.

2.19 "Working Day" means any day other than Saturday, Sunday, or a federal holiday observed
in the Commonwealth of Virginia.

2.20 "Assessment Report" means any written deliverable produced by the Service Provider in
connection with the performance of assessment services under this Agreement, including
without limitation penetration test reports, vulnerability assessment reports, compliance gap
analyses, and advisory communications.

2.21 "Confidential Information" means all non-public information disclosed by either party
to the other, whether orally, in writing, by electronic transmission, or by inspection,
including but not limited to financial data, customer information, system configurations,
network architecture details, vulnerability data, security assessment findings, and
business operations information.

2.22 "Control Assessment" means the systematic evaluation of the design adequacy and operating
effectiveness of security controls implemented within the Client's information systems and
processes, mapped to applicable regulatory requirements and industry standard control
frameworks as described in Exhibit C.

3. SCOPE OF SERVICES

3.1 Comprehensive Cybersecurity Assessment. The Service Provider shall provide comprehensive
cybersecurity assessment services encompassing the following core service areas:

    (a) Penetration Testing. The Service Provider shall conduct network penetration testing,
    application penetration testing, and wireless network penetration testing across the
    Client's internal and external-facing systems in accordance with the Rules of Engagement
    set forth in Exhibit A. Penetration testing shall be conducted using a combination of
    automated vulnerability exploitation tools and manual testing techniques to identify
    exploitable vulnerabilities, misconfigurations, and security control gaps. All testing
    shall be performed in a controlled manner with defined escalation procedures to prevent
    disruption to production systems.

    (b) Vulnerability Scanning. The Service Provider shall perform recurring vulnerability
    scanning of the Client's network infrastructure, server environments, web applications,
    database systems, and endpoint devices using industry-standard scanning tools. Scanning
    shall include authenticated and unauthenticated scanning methodologies, with results
    correlated and de-duplicated to provide an accurate view of the Client's vulnerability
    posture. Vulnerability scans shall be conducted on a monthly basis during the term of
    this Agreement.

    (c) Social Engineering Assessments. The Service Provider shall conduct periodic social
    engineering assessments designed to evaluate the effectiveness of the Client's security
    awareness program and the susceptibility of Client personnel to common social engineering
    attack vectors. Assessments shall include simulated phishing campaigns (email-based,
    SMS-based, and voice-based), pretexting scenarios targeting physical access controls,
    and USB drop tests. All social engineering assessments shall be conducted with appropriate
    safeguards to prevent unauthorized disclosure of test activities to Client personnel who
    are not designated as test targets.

    (d) Security Architecture Review. The Service Provider shall perform a comprehensive
    review of the Client's security architecture, including network segmentation design,
    firewall rule sets, intrusion detection and prevention system configurations, endpoint
    protection deployment, identity and access management implementation, data loss prevention
    controls, and cloud security configurations. The architecture review shall evaluate the
    alignment of the Client's security infrastructure with industry best practices and
    applicable regulatory requirements.

    (e) Regulatory Compliance Gap Analysis. The Service Provider shall conduct a thorough
    gap analysis of the Client's compliance posture with respect to the Gramm-Leach-Bliley
    Act (GLBA) Safeguards Rule, the Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX) information technology general
    controls requirements, and the Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard (PCI DSS)
    version 4.0. The gap analysis shall identify areas of non-compliance, partial compliance,
    and full compliance, with specific remediation recommendations prioritized by risk
    severity and regulatory impact. The regulatory mapping framework used for this analysis
    is described in Exhibit C.

    (f) Incident Response Readiness Evaluation. The Service Provider shall evaluate the
    Client's incident response capabilities, including the adequacy of the Client's incident
    response plan, the completeness and currency of contact lists and escalation procedures,
    the availability and testing of forensic tools and capabilities, the frequency and
    realism of incident response tabletop exercises and simulations, and the effectiveness
    of communication protocols with internal stakeholders, external legal counsel,
    regulatory agencies, and law enforcement.

    (g) Security Awareness Program Development. The Service Provider shall assist the Client
    in the development and enhancement of its security awareness program, including the
    design of role-based training curricula, the creation of assessment mechanisms to measure
    training effectiveness, and the establishment of metrics and benchmarks for continuous
    improvement of the Client's human-centric security posture.

3.2 Ongoing Monitoring and Advisory. In addition to the assessment services described in
Section 3.1, the Service Provider shall provide ongoing monitoring and advisory services
throughout the term of this Agreement, including monthly vulnerability scanning and
remediation tracking, quarterly security posture reviews, ad-hoc advisory support for
emerging threat intelligence, and guidance on security control implementations and
configurations.

4. ASSESSMENT METHODOLOGY

4.1 NIST Cybersecurity Framework. All assessment services performed under this Agreement shall
be conducted in alignment with the NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) version 2.0, organized
across the six core functions: Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, and Recover.
Assessment findings shall be mapped to relevant CSF subcategories and implementation tiers
to provide the Client with a comprehensive view of its cybersecurity maturity relative to
the framework's target profile.

4.2 OWASP Testing Methodology. Application-layer penetration testing and web application
vulnerability assessments shall be conducted in accordance with the Open Worldwide Application
Security Project (OWASP) Testing Guide methodology, covering information gathering,
configuration management testing, identity management testing, authentication testing,
authorization testing, session management testing, input validation testing, error handling
testing, cryptography testing, business logic testing, and client-side testing.

4.3 MITRE ATT&CK Framework. Penetration testing scenarios and threat emulation exercises
shall be mapped to the MITRE ATT&CK framework to provide the Client with threat-based
context for identified vulnerabilities and attack paths. Testing activities shall be aligned
with relevant ATT&CK tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs) based on the threat
landscape most relevant to the financial services sector.

4.4 Rules of Engagement. All penetration testing and security assessment activities shall be
conducted in strict accordance with the Rules of Engagement set forth in Exhibit A. The
Rules of Engagement define the scope of testing, authorized targets, prohibited activities,
testing windows, communication procedures, escalation paths, and emergency contact
information. Any deviation from the Rules of Engagement shall require prior written approval
from both parties.

7. DELIVERABLES AND MILESTONES

7.1 Penetration Test Report. The Service Provider shall deliver a comprehensive penetration
test report to the Client within sixty (60) calendar days of the commencement of penetration
testing activities. The report shall include an executive summary, detailed technical findings
organized by severity, step-by-step exploitation narratives for each confirmed vulnerability,
evidence of exploitation including screenshots and system output, and prioritized remediation
recommendations with estimated effort and complexity assessments.

7.2 Vulnerability Assessment Report. The Service Provider shall deliver the initial
vulnerability assessment report to the Client within forty-five (45) calendar days of the
completion of the initial vulnerability scan cycle. The report shall present all identified
vulnerabilities with associated CVSS scores, affected assets, patch availability information,
and risk-ranked remediation priorities.

7.3 Compliance Gap Analysis Report. The Service Provider shall deliver the regulatory
compliance gap analysis report to the Client within ninety (90) calendar days of the Effective
Date. The report shall map the Client's current control environment against the requirements
of GLBA, SOX, and PCI DSS, identifying specific gaps and providing detailed remediation
roadmaps with resource and timeline estimates. The regulatory mapping framework used for this
analysis is attached hereto as Exhibit C.

7.4 Incident Response Plan. The Service Provider shall deliver a comprehensive incident
response readiness evaluation report, including recommended enhancements to the Client's
incident response plan, within one hundred twenty (120) calendar days of the Effective Date.
The deliverable shall include a revised incident response plan template, communication
templates for various incident categories, forensic investigation procedures, and a tabletop
exercise scenario library.

7.5 Quarterly Advisory Reports. The Service Provider shall deliver quarterly security posture
advisory reports to the Client on an ongoing basis throughout the term of this Agreement.
Each quarterly report shall summarize vulnerability scan results and remediation progress,
emerging threat intelligence relevant to the financial services sector, security control
recommendations, and a comparison of the Client's security posture against industry benchmarks
and peer institutions.

9.1 Report Classification. All Assessment Reports shall be classified in accordance with the
following sensitivity categories: CONFIDENTIAL — RESTRICTED (findings that could directly
enable unauthorized access to Client systems), CONFIDENTIAL (general security assessment
findings and recommendations), and INTERNAL (summary information suitable for broader
distribution within the Client's organization). Each report shall bear the appropriate
classification marking on every page.

9.2 Report Format. Assessment Reports shall be delivered in PDF format, with technical
appendices provided in both PDF and Microsoft Excel formats where applicable. Reports shall
include a cover page identifying the Service Provider, the Client, the assessment type,
the assessment period, and the report classification.

9.3 Report Distribution. Assessment Reports classified as CONFIDENTIAL — RESTRICTED shall
be distributed only to the Client's Chief Information Security Officer, Chief Information
Officer, and such other personnel as the Client's CISO may designate in writing. Assessment
Reports classified as CONFIDENTIAL may be distributed to the Client's senior management team,
legal counsel, and board-level risk committee members. INTERNAL reports may be distributed
more broadly within the Client's organization at the Client's discretion.

employee, agent, partner, or joint venturer of the Client. Nothing in this Agreement shall
be construed to create an employment relationship, partnership, joint venture, or agency
relationship between the parties.

18.8 Force Majeure. Neither party shall be liable for any failure or delay in the performance
of its obligations under this Agreement to the extent that such failure or delay is caused by
circumstances beyond the reasonable control of the affected party, including but not limited
to acts of God, natural disasters, pandemics, government actions, labor disputes, civil
unrest, war, terrorism, cyberattacks by third parties not attributable to the affected party's
negligence, and power or telecommunications failures.

18.9 Subcontractor Management. The Service Provider shall not engage any subcontractor to
perform services under this Agreement without the prior written consent of the Client. Any
approved subcontractor shall be subject to the same confidentiality, security, and quality
standards as the Service Provider, and the Service Provider shall remain fully responsible
for the performance of any subcontracted services.

18.10 Records Retention. The Service Provider shall maintain all records, working papers,
scan results, and test data generated in connection with the performance of services under
this Agreement for a period of not less than seven (7) years following the completion of the
applicable assessment engagement, or such longer period as may be required by applicable law
or regulation.

18.11 Regulatory Examination Support. The Service Provider shall, upon the Client's reasonable
request and at the Client's expense, make personnel available to participate in regulatory
examinations, audits, or inquiries conducted by federal or state banking regulators, the
Payment Card Industry Security Standards Council, or other regulatory bodies with jurisdiction
over the Client's operations, to the extent such participation relates to assessment services
performed under this Agreement.

18.12 Data Handling Procedures. All Client data accessed, processed, or stored by the Service
Provider in connection with the performance of services under this Agreement shall be handled
in accordance with the Client's data classification policy and the data handling requirements
specified in the Rules of Engagement (Exhibit A). The Service Provider shall implement
appropriate technical and organizational measures to protect Client data, including encryption
of data at rest and in transit, access controls based on the principle of least privilege,
and secure deletion procedures for data no longer required for the performance of services.
